CHAPTER 11

🧠 Types of Reasoning in AI

Nine Reasoning Paradigms You Need to Know

Learning Objectives:
Understand 9 distinct types of human reasoning and how to implement them in AI systems: deductive, inductive, abductive, analogical, causal, probabilistic, practical, argumentative, and metacognitive reasoning.

Introduction: Why Reasoning Types Matter

Not all thinking is the same. Humans reason in fundamentally different ways depending on the problem: proving theorems uses different logic than diagnosing disease, which differs from learning from examples, which differs from planning actions. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for building AI systems that reason the way humans do.

1. Deductive Reasoning: From General to Specific

Definition:
Deductive reasoning starts with general premises and derives conclusions that must be logically true if the premises are true. The conclusion is necessarily true, not probabilistic.

Form:

Premise 1: All humans are mortal
Premise 2: Socrates is human
Conclusion: Socrates is mortal

This is ALWAYS true if premises are true (valid deduction)

Medical Deduction Example

Rule: "If patient has symptom A and symptom B and symptom C, then they definitely have condition X"
Patient presents with: Symptom A, Symptom B, Symptom C
Conclusion: Patient has condition X (certain, not probable)

2. Inductive Reasoning: From Specific to General

Definition:
Inductive reasoning generalizes from specific examples/observations to a broader rule. Conclusions are probable, not certain.

Form:

Observation 1: Patient A has symptom X and recovered with drug D
Observation 2: Patient B has symptom X and recovered with drug D
Observation 3: Patient C has symptom X and recovered with drug D
...
Generalization: Patients with symptom X usually respond to drug D

This is PROBABLE (could always be wrong), not certain

Machine Learning is Inductive Reasoning

You train a neural network on thousands of examples (specific observations), and it learns a general pattern (rule). The learned model makes predictions probabilistically. This is pure induction!

3. Abductive Reasoning: Finding the Best Explanation

Definition:
Abductive reasoning infers the most plausible explanation for incomplete or noisy observations. It's the reasoning doctors use for diagnosis: "Given these symptoms, what's the most likely disease?"

Form:

Observation: Patient has fever, cough, sore throat, fatigue
Possible Explanations:
  - Flu (probability: 0.70)
  - Cold (probability: 0.20)
  - Strep Throat (probability: 0.07)
  - COVID-19 (probability: 0.03)

Abductive Conclusion: Most likely diagnosis is Flu (highest probability)

Note: Not certain! Could be wrong. But best guess given evidence.

Diagnostic Reasoning

Clinicians don't have complete information. They see symptoms and must infer the underlying condition. This is pure abduction: given incomplete information, find the most likely explanation.

4. Analogical Reasoning: Transfer from Similar Situations

Definition:
Analogical reasoning transfers structure and solutions from a known situation (source) to a new situation (target) by mapping correspondences.

Form:

Source Situation: A patient with disease A responds well to drug X
Mapping: This new patient's disease B resembles disease A in structure
Target Situation: Maybe this patient should also try drug X?

Analogy reasoning: "The situations are similar, so maybe the solution is similar"

Analogical Medical Reasoning

"I've never seen this rare disease before, but it resembles Condition Q in these 5 ways. I know Condition Q responds to treatment Z. So maybe this disease would too."

5. Causal Reasoning: Understanding Cause and Effect

Definition:
Causal reasoning understands cause-effect relationships and can predict consequences of interventions. It answers "What if?" questions.

Form:

Causal Knowledge:
  - Taking antibiotic CAUSES bacteria to die
  - Bacteria death CAUSES symptom improvement

Intervention: Patient starts antibiotic
Prediction: Symptoms will improve (because of causal chain)

Counterfactual: "If patient had NOT taken antibiotic, symptoms would worsen"

Treatment Planning with Causal Reasoning

"Why give treatment X? Because it causes outcome Y, which alleviates the root cause of the disease." Causal knowledge enables planning: we choose actions because we understand their causal consequences.

6. Probabilistic/Statistical Reasoning: Reasoning Under Uncertainty

Definition:
Probabilistic reasoning updates beliefs under uncertainty using likelihoods, base rates, and Bayesian inference.

Form (Bayes' Theorem):

P(Disease | Symptoms) = P(Symptoms | Disease) × P(Disease) / P(Symptoms)

Example:
- Base rate: 1% of population has Disease X
- Symptom sensitivity: 95% of disease X patients show the symptom
- Symptom specificity: 10% of non-disease people show the symptom

Patient shows symptom. What's probability they have Disease X?
P(X | symptom) = (0.95 × 0.01) / ((0.95 × 0.01) + (0.10 × 0.99))
              = 0.0095 / 0.1085 = 8.8%

Even though test is 95% sensitive, disease probability is only 8.8%!
(Because disease is rare in population)

Clinical Decision with Uncertainty

A screening test comes back positive. But you must account for base rate: how common is the disease in the general population? A rare disease + positive test doesn't automatically mean patient has it. Bayesian reasoning handles this correctly.

7. Practical Reasoning: Planning and Decision-Making

Definition:
Practical reasoning chooses actions to achieve goals under constraints, trade-offs, and incomplete information. It's the reasoning behind "What should I do?"

Form:

Goal: Help child recover from illness
Constraints: Limited time, budget, expertise
Available Actions:
  - Watch and wait (low cost, slow)
  - Visit doctor (high cost, fast)
  - Go to ER (very high cost, immediate)

Trade-offs:
  - Waiting is cheaper but may miss critical condition
  - ER visit is definitive but costly and unnecessary if mild

Practical Reasoning: Choose "visit doctor" as best balance given:
  - Severity of symptoms (moderate)
  - Time constraints (not immediately life-threatening)
  - Cost considerations
  - Urgency

8. Argumentative Reasoning: Constructing and Evaluating Arguments

Definition:
Argumentative reasoning constructs, evaluates, and rebuts arguments. It's how we justify conclusions to others and to ourselves.

Form:

Claim: "This patient should start antibiotics immediately"

Argument Structure:
  Premise 1: Patient has fever and cough
  Premise 2: Fever + cough suggests bacterial infection
  Premise 3: Early antibiotic intervention improves outcomes
  Conclusion: Therefore, start antibiotics immediately

Counterargument:
  "Wait—fever + cough could be viral. Unnecessary antibiotics cause resistance.
   Let's do a culture first to confirm bacterial infection."

Rebuttal:
  "Valid point, but patient is elderly with comorbidities. Waiting for culture
   results (48 hours) is too risky. Empiric antibiotics justified here."

9. Metacognitive Reasoning: Thinking About Thinking

Definition:
Metacognitive reasoning is self-awareness about one's own thinking. It monitors, evaluates, and regulates your reasoning process.

Form:

Scenario: You're making a diagnostic decision

Metacognitive Monitoring:
  - "Am I confident in my reasoning?"
  - "What evidence am I basing this on?"
  - "What could I be missing?"
  - "Is there a simpler explanation I overlooked?"

Metacognitive Control:
  - "My confidence is 60%. That's too low. I should consult another expert."
  - "I'm seeing pattern that confirms my hypothesis. Am I cherry-picking evidence?"
  - "I should double-check the red flags before finalizing my decision."

Result: Better decisions through self-awareness about reasoning limitations

Comparison: All 9 Types

Type Direction Certainty Use Case AI Implementation
Deductive General → Specific Certain Applying rules to facts Production systems, logic engines
Inductive Specific → General Probable Learning patterns from data Machine learning, neural networks
Abductive Observation → Best Explanation Plausible Diagnosis, anomaly detection Probabilistic inference, Bayesian models
Analogical Similar → Transfer Solution Possible Novel problem solving Case-based reasoning, similarity metrics
Causal Cause → Effect Conditional Prediction, intervention planning Causal graphs, structural causal models
Probabilistic Evidence → Updated Belief Quantified Decision under uncertainty Bayesian networks, probabilistic models
Practical Goals + Constraints → Action Pragmatic Decision-making, planning Planning algorithms, MDP, reinforcement learning
Argumentative Claims → Justification Persuasive Explanation, debate Natural language generation, argument mining
Metacognitive Reasoning → Self-Assessment Evaluative Error checking, confidence calibration Uncertainty estimation, confidence scoring

Implementing Reasoning Types in Neuro-Symbolic Systems

A trustworthy AI system often needs multiple reasoning types working together:

Medical Diagnosis System Using All 9 Types

  1. Deductive: "If patient meets criteria A, B, C → they have disease X" (applying diagnostic rules)
  2. Inductive: ML model trained on 10,000 cases learns symptom patterns
  3. Abductive: "Given these symptoms, most likely diagnosis is Y (not Z)"
  4. Analogical: "This rare case resembles a past case I've seen; use similar treatment"
  5. Causal: "Giving antibiotic will kill bacteria → symptom improvement"
  6. Probabilistic: "Base rate of disease is 5%. Test is 90% sensitive. Patient probability: 32%"
  7. Practical: "Given 4 possible treatments, which balances efficacy, side effects, and cost?"
  8. Argumentative: "Justify the decision: here's why this treatment despite these risks"
  9. Metacognitive: "I'm 65% confident. Too low. Need to escalate to human expert."

Key Takeaways

  • Different problems require different reasoning types—don't assume one approach works for all
  • Deductive reasoning is certain but limited to what you can derive from premises
  • Inductive reasoning (ML) is powerful for learning but probabilistic, not certain
  • Abductive reasoning (diagnosis) finds best explanation from incomplete information
  • Causal reasoning enables prediction and planning by understanding interventions
  • Probabilistic reasoning handles uncertainty correctly using Bayesian logic
  • Practical reasoning balances goals, constraints, and trade-offs
  • Argumentative reasoning justifies decisions (crucial for trust and transparency)
  • Metacognitive reasoning self-monitors and catches errors (safety mechanism)
  • Robust AI systems often blend multiple reasoning types, not rely on just one
